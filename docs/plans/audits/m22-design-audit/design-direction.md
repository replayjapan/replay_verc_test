# M22 Design Direction

Creative direction for the Japanese-language premium domain portfolio and digital services site on `feature/m22-site-design-inspection`.

This brief is informed by premium minimal references surfaced through:

- Godly features such as `NONYMOUS`, `AndAgain`, `Motto`, and `Kaleido Grafik`
- Awwwards’ `Minimal` collection, including references such as Stewart Partners’ landing page, Dash Digital’s clean graphic design / identity strategy work, Antinomy Studio Portfolio, Seedlip, and Marmo Elite
- Dead Simple Websites as a reminder that clarity can come from ruthless reduction, though not as a luxury visual benchmark

The key conclusion is simple: the site does not need more cards. It needs more authorship.

## 1. Design anti-patterns to stop using

- Stop solving every section with a bordered rectangle on a white page. That pattern makes the site feel like a CMS inventory screen, not a premium brand.
- Stop using generic one-line intros such as “一覧です” or “ご覧ください” as if the layout alone will create value. Premium sites frame the content with a point of view.
- Stop treating every content type with the same visual weight. Services, domains, articles, videos, and company information should not all look like equivalent index cards.
- Stop relying on pale metadata. Dates, categories, status labels, and secondary copy are repeatedly too weak to support trust at a glance.
- Stop using empty white space as a substitute for composition. Space can feel luxurious, but only when typography and contrast are doing deliberate work. Right now much of the emptiness reads as absence, not restraint.
- Stop making CTAs visually polite. This business sells assets worth millions of yen. Inquiry actions should look decisive, not apologetic.
- Stop presenting high-value inventory like a spreadsheet or admin table. A premium asset list can still be functional, but it must feel selected, ranked, and valuable.
- Stop using icon-card triptychs as the default answer for “about us” or “why choose us.” That pattern is overused and too small-minded for the ambition of the brand.

## 2. Design principles to adopt

### 1. Typography is the primary design surface

The best references, especially `NONYMOUS`, `AndAgain`, `Motto`, and many entries in Awwwards’ `Minimal` collection, create authority through type before decoration. Large Japanese headlines, crisp subheads, disciplined line lengths, and deliberate contrast should carry the experience. Sections should be memorable because the language is staged powerfully, not because another card was added.

### 2. Contrast should define section identity

Section transitions should come from meaningful shifts in tone, not minor gray tint changes. The strongest references alternate light and dark with confidence, or create transitions through scale, image density, and whitespace compression. Every major section should have a reason to feel different from the one before it.

### 3. Space is a luxury signal only when it is tense and intentional

Premium minimal work does not just leave blank areas. It uses wide margins, asymmetry, and controlled emptiness to make type and imagery feel expensive. The site should use fewer but stronger objects per screen. One strong statement with room around it is better than three mediocre cards.

### 4. Inventory should feel curated, not dumped

Whether the user is browsing domains, services, articles, or portfolio items, the experience should feel guided. The strongest premium sites edit aggressively: featured items, ranked items, purposeful rhythm, and clear reasons to care. The visitor should feel that selection itself is part of the brand’s intelligence.

### 5. Utility pages still need ceremony

Luxury and premium agency sites do not abandon design discipline on search, contact, privacy, or detail pages. They become calmer, not generic. Forms, tables, and informational sections should still feel authored through typography, spacing, and contrast.

## 3. Page-by-page creative direction

## Homepage

The homepage should feel like entering a discreet, high-value firm: calm, assured, expensive, and specific. The current hero already carries some of this. What is missing is the experience after the hero. Instead of dropping into a white card catalog, the page should move through a sequence of clearly authored scenes.

After the hero, the user should encounter a tight, highly curated domain showcase that feels like a gallery of assets, not a product carousel. Then the page should shift into an editorial statement about the firm: not three icon cards, but a typographic “why us” moment. Think of the authority found in `NONYMOUS` or `AndAgain`: a bold sentence or two, strong Japanese typography, restrained proof points, and perhaps one supporting image or one compact data line. The “私たちについて” section should feel like a thesis, not a feature grid.

The inquiry moment should feel more like a private consultation invitation than a newsletter bar. It should read as a decisive commercial step with strong contrast and breathing room, not a small utility strip.

Most important: the homepage below the hero needs real transitions. Not `gray-50`. Use dark-to-light shifts, editorial full-width statements, image interruptions, and compression/expansion of space. The visitor should feel a sequence of scenes, not a vertical stack of containers.

## About

The About page should feel like an elegant firm profile. The user should quickly understand: who this company is, what kind of clients it serves, what kind of judgment it brings, and why it can be trusted with high-value digital assets.

The first screen after the hero should stage a strong positioning statement in large type, followed by a short, dense supporting paragraph. Structured company facts such as address, phone, capital, and company profile should not be buried in body copy. They should be presented as calm, precise facts, almost like a discreet prospectus.

The middle of the page should alternate between one strong image-led moment and one text-led moment. `Kaleido Grafik` and several SiteInspire-style agency references suggest a quieter editorial rhythm: fewer objects, larger type, more deliberate asymmetry, less “content block” energy.

The end of the page should feel like closing a brand book, not scrolling through a FAQ. The company timeline or trust markers should be presented with gravity and spacing, not like a default accordion module.

## Domains listing

The domains page should feel like a premium asset inventory, somewhere between a private catalogue and an investment overview. It should not feel like a marketplace spreadsheet.

The top of the page should frame the collection in commercial language: why these domains matter, what makes them rare, what kind of buyer they suit. This is where the site should feel closer to luxury retail or financial curation than to a filterable CMS table.

Featured domains should feel staged, almost like hero assets in a gallery. Then the full inventory should shift into a more disciplined tabular browse mode, but the domain name must dominate. The eye should land on the name first, then price, then category/status. Right now the list spreads attention too evenly across columns.

If there are 47 domains, the page should feel like a curated ledger. Use typography, spacing, and ranking to make the table feel expensive. Think less “admin list,” more “catalogue raisonné.” Awwwards luxury references such as `Seedlip` and `Marmo Elite` are useful here because they prove that quiet layouts can still feel expensive when hierarchy is sharp.

## Domain detail

This page should feel like the dossier for a single high-value asset. The visitor should feel they are looking at a premium opportunity, not just a record.

The opening summary should be clean and severe: domain name, price, classification, and trust markers with strong typographic hierarchy. The inquiry path should feel immediate and unmissable. A fast-scanning buyer should instantly know how to proceed.

The descriptive sections below should read like a sales memorandum: what makes the domain valuable, how it can be used, why the category matters, and what adjacent assets are relevant. The current structure is on the right track, but the inquiry area still feels too similar to everything else. For this page, commercial intent must be visible.

Related domains should feel like intelligent recommendations, not generic “more cards.”

## Services listing

Premium agencies do not usually present services as a dead-flat grid of equivalent boxes. They present them as a strategic offer.

This page should open with a strong point of view about the firm’s role in the market, followed by one or two featured capabilities expressed with more scale. The remaining services can then appear in a quieter list or editorial grid. `Motto`, `AndAgain`, and many agency references on Godly rely on scale shifts and typographic authority rather than a wall of identical service tiles.

The visitor should feel expertise, not options overload. Services should feel like a suite of capabilities under one worldview. Less directory, more discipline.

## Service detail

The service detail page should feel like a persuasive brief for a premium engagement. The hero can stay visually strong, but the rest of the page needs to move beyond plain tabbed content.

The page should alternate between explanation, proof, and invitation. One section can be all type. Another can compress into dark contrast with metrics or commitments. Another can show a process or scenario in a spacious editorial layout. The final CTA should feel like an invitation to start a serious conversation, not a small footer strip.

The user should leave with the impression that this company has a method, not just a service description.

## Blog

The blog should feel more like a journal than a card feed. The current featured article direction is correct, but it should go further.

The page needs a short editorial framing paragraph at the top: what the publication covers, why it exists, and what kind of thinking the reader will find. Then the first item should feel clearly featured, with the remaining posts receding into a secondary rhythm. Awwwards editorial minimal references and quieter SiteInspire patterns show that publication pages feel premium when the first story is clearly staged and the rest are deliberately paced.

Excerpt handling should be visibly disciplined. Japanese summaries need consistent editorial length so the page feels designed, not incidental.

## Blog post

The post page should feel like a serious editorial read. The opening hero can remain image-led, but the body needs more rhythm and gravity.

Instead of feeling like “hero then plain article,” the page should feel like a composed reading environment. Large inter-headings, strong paragraph rhythm, subtle pull moments, and clearly separated sections would help. The user should feel that the company thinks clearly, not just publishes content.

For a premium Japanese-language site, reading comfort is part of the brand.

## Articles listing

This page should feel like a knowledge library, not a near-empty index. If the content count is small, that should feel selective, not sparse.

The top should frame the library as expertise: explain what kind of documents or insights live here and why they matter. Then the first article can take a featured treatment similar to a white-paper cover or editorial lead story. If the archive grows, the page should transition into grouped or topic-based browsing, not just a repeated stack.

Again: fewer containers, more hierarchy.

## Article detail

This page should feel closer to a premium report than a standard blog template. Wider margins, stronger title handling, quieter metadata, and more deliberate body rhythm would help.

The article should feel like a document worth reading and citing. That means calm authority, not decorative flourish.

## Videos listing

The video page currently feels like an embed gallery. It should feel like a curated media program.

It needs a meaningful introduction that explains the kinds of videos available and why they matter. The first row should feel selected. Cards should feel more editorial than YouTube-derived. The visitor should understand immediately whether the videos are educational, strategic, or promotional.

The visual system should do more to tame noisy thumbnails. Think of the calm editorial framing seen in the stronger Godly references: typography and spacing should discipline the media, not surrender to it.

## Video detail

The video detail page should feel like a media article with a film at its center, not just a video embed plus caption.

There should be a sense of occasion around the video: title, context, why to watch, and what the viewer will get from it. Supporting material should feel integrated into a narrative, not tacked underneath.

## Portfolio listing

The portfolio should feel like selected work, not a project directory. The top of the page should say what kind of work the firm does and what standard it holds itself to.

Projects should be framed around value and transformation, not just title and tool tags. The grid should feel curated. If the item count leaves an awkward last row, the layout should feel intentionally asymmetrical rather than mechanically incomplete. Premium studios often allow one project to take more room or more emphasis, especially at the bottom of a set.

## Portfolio detail

This page should feel like a case study, not an object record. The visitor should understand the challenge, the intervention, and the outcome.

The current page is too thin. It needs a narrative arc. The user should feel they are being shown evidence of judgment and execution. That is how premium agencies build trust.

The visual experience should be closer to a short editorial story than a product detail page.

## Contact

Premium sites treat contact as a final act of confidence. The current form feels exposed and under-hierarchized.

The contact page should feel calm, private, and guided. The company should first reassure the visitor that they are in the right place, then present contact routes with clarity, then transition into a well-framed form. The form itself should not look like thin lines floating in empty space. It should feel like a composed interaction area with visible hierarchy, generous field spacing, and one unmistakable submit action.

Think of the way premium sites stage forms as part of a conversation, not as a utility dump.

## Search

The dark header direction is correct. Search should feel like a productized internal tool, not a blank page with tabs.

The top should stay calm and authoritative, then the lower state should feel informative even before results appear. Suggested searches, category framing, or highlighted content should make the page feel alive. Minimal product pages in the stronger reference sets often use a single dark field plus quiet data below; that is a more premium direction than another pale block.

## Privacy

This page can remain restrained, but it should still feel authored. Legal does not need drama; it needs calm clarity.

The main lesson here is not to over-design it. Use strong typography, good measure, and calm spacing.

## 4. Typography and contrast system

### Heading scale

- Use fewer heading sizes, but make the top two much more decisive.
- Primary page and section headlines should feel editorial and expensive, with enough size difference from body text that they define the page rhythm on their own.
- On mobile, keep the same authority but remove the tendency toward oversized, cramped hero lines. Japanese display type needs room to breathe.

### Body text

- Body text should be slightly darker and slightly denser than it is now.
- Japanese reading comfort should be prioritized over airy “light gray minimalism.”
- Long paragraphs should feel deliberate and literate, not washed out.

### Metadata

- Dates, categories, badges, and status labels should be quieter than headings, but still legible at a glance.
- Premium does not mean faint. Secondary information should feel crisp, controlled, and intentional.
- Domain status and price labels especially should be treated as commercial signals, not decorative chips.

### Contrast rhythm

- Build a clear light/dark cadence across long pages.
- Use dark sections for authority, compression, and conversion.
- Use light sections for reading, product inspection, and calm breathing space.
- Avoid endless white stacks separated only by margins.

### Type pairing and tone

- The site should feel more editorial and less app-like.
- Favor a refined grotesk/sans with disciplined Japanese support, and use scale and weight rather than extra boxes to create emphasis.
- English accents such as `SEO` or `Marketing` are acceptable, but they should feel intentional and sparse, more like labeling than decoration.

## 5. Things to keep

- The warm orange accent is working. It gives the brand energy without becoming loud.
- The strongest hero imagery works when paired with dark overlays and confident type. That basic opening language should stay.
- The domain cards themselves have a useful commercial hierarchy, especially where price and status are clear.
- The move toward darker contrast on Search and the metrics band on the service detail page is a good direction. Those moments prove the system benefits from stronger tonal identity.
- The overall restraint is correct. The solution is not to become flashy or overloaded. The solution is to become more intentional, more typographic, and more selective.
